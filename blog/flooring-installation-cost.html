New Flooring Cost 2026: Every Type Compared

Updated February 2026 · 12 min read

New flooring transforms the look and feel of your entire home, but costs range from surprisingly affordable to eye-wateringly expensive depending on the material you choose. Whether you are weighing luxury vinyl plank against hardwood or debating tile versus carpet, this guide breaks down the installed cost of every major flooring type in 2026, along with the hidden expenses most homeowners overlook.

Quick Cost Summary by Flooring Type

These are fully installed prices including material, labor, and basic prep. Actual costs vary by region, subfloor condition, and room complexity.

Carpet $3 — $11 / sq ft
Laminate $3 — $8 / sq ft
Luxury Vinyl Plank (LVP) $4 — $9 / sq ft
Engineered Hardwood $6 — $14 / sq ft
Ceramic Tile $5 — $12 / sq ft
Porcelain Tile $7 — $16 / sq ft
Solid Hardwood $8 — $18 / sq ft
Natural Stone (marble, slate, travertine) $12 — $22 / sq ft

Solid Hardwood Flooring

Solid hardwood remains the gold standard for flooring and continues to be the most requested material among homeowners in 2026. Planks are milled from a single piece of wood, typically three-quarters of an inch thick, and can be sanded and refinished multiple times over a lifespan of 50 to 100 years. Popular species include red oak, white oak, hickory, maple, and walnut, each with distinct grain patterns and hardness ratings.

Material costs for solid hardwood run $4 to $10 per square foot depending on the species and grade. White oak is the most popular species in 2026 due to its durability and compatibility with modern stain colors. Professional installation adds $4 to $8 per square foot, bringing the total installed cost to $8 to $18 per square foot. Exotic hardwoods like Brazilian cherry or teak push the high end past $20 per square foot installed. The main drawback of solid hardwood is its sensitivity to moisture. It is not recommended for basements, bathrooms, or areas with high humidity because the wood will expand, cup, and buckle over time.

Engineered Hardwood Flooring

Engineered hardwood features a real wood veneer layer bonded to a plywood or high-density fiberboard core. This layered construction makes it more dimensionally stable than solid hardwood, meaning it handles humidity fluctuations and temperature changes much better. Most engineered planks have a veneer thickness of 2 to 6 millimeters, and higher-quality products with thicker veneers can be sanded and refinished one to three times.

Engineered hardwood costs $3 to $8 per square foot for materials and $3 to $6 per square foot for professional installation, totaling $6 to $14 per square foot installed. It works well over radiant heat systems and can be installed on concrete slabs where solid hardwood cannot. For homeowners who want the authentic look and feel of real wood flooring at a lower price point, engineered hardwood hits the sweet spot. Brands like Shaw, Mohawk, and Bruce offer wide-plank white oak engineered options in the $7 to $10 per square foot installed range that are nearly indistinguishable from solid hardwood.

Luxury Vinyl Plank (LVP)

Luxury vinyl plank has exploded in popularity over the past five years, and in 2026 it is the single most installed flooring type in the United States. LVP is 100 percent waterproof, extremely durable, comfortable underfoot, and available in remarkably realistic wood and stone patterns. Higher-end LVP products feature rigid SPC (stone polymer composite) cores that resist denting and provide a solid feel underfoot that older vinyl products never achieved.

LVP material costs range from $2 to $5 per square foot, with premium brands like COREtec, LifeProof, and Mohawk RevWood at the higher end. Professional installation runs $2 to $4 per square foot, putting total installed costs at $4 to $9 per square foot. Many LVP products feature click-lock installation systems that make them excellent candidates for DIY installation, potentially saving you 40 to 60 percent on labor. LVP works in every room of the house including basements and bathrooms. The main drawback is that it cannot be refinished. When the wear layer is worn through (typically after 15 to 25 years), the flooring must be replaced entirely.

Laminate Flooring

Laminate flooring uses a photographic image layer over a high-density fiberboard core, topped with a clear aluminum oxide wear layer. Modern laminate from brands like Pergo, Mohawk, and Quick-Step has improved dramatically and can mimic wood grain with impressive realism. Laminate is one of the most budget-friendly flooring options available, costing $1 to $4 per square foot for materials and $2 to $4 per square foot for installation, putting the total installed cost at $3 to $8 per square foot.

The biggest limitation of traditional laminate is water sensitivity. The fiberboard core swells when exposed to standing water, which rules it out for bathrooms and laundry rooms. However, waterproof laminate products have entered the market in recent years, using polymer or stone composite cores instead of fiberboard. These waterproof laminates cost slightly more at $4 to $7 per square foot installed but expand laminate's usability to wet areas. Laminate typically lasts 15 to 25 years and cannot be refinished, so it will eventually need full replacement.

Porcelain Tile

Porcelain tile is fired at higher temperatures than ceramic, making it denser, harder, and more water-resistant. It is ideal for bathrooms, kitchens, entryways, and mudrooms where moisture and heavy foot traffic are concerns. Porcelain is also one of the few flooring types rated for outdoor use in freeze-thaw climates. Large-format porcelain tiles that mimic natural stone or wood planks are trending strongly in 2026, with sizes like 12 by 48 inches and 24 by 48 inches creating a sleek, modern look with fewer grout lines.

Porcelain tile materials cost $3 to $8 per square foot, but installation labor is significantly higher than most other flooring types at $4 to $8 per square foot. Tile installation requires skilled labor for proper layout, cutting, leveling, thinset application, and grouting. The total installed cost of $7 to $16 per square foot reflects this labor intensity. Porcelain tile lasts 50 to 75 years or more with proper maintenance, making it one of the best long-term value propositions despite the higher upfront cost.

Ceramic Tile

Ceramic tile is the more affordable cousin of porcelain. It is made from natural clay fired at lower temperatures, resulting in a softer, more porous product. Ceramic works well in bathrooms, kitchens, and laundry rooms but is not as durable as porcelain in high-traffic commercial settings. Ceramic tile materials run $1 to $5 per square foot, with professional installation adding $4 to $7 per square foot. Total installed cost lands at $5 to $12 per square foot.

The main advantages of ceramic over porcelain are lower cost and easier cutting during installation. Ceramic tiles are softer and can be cut with basic tile saws, while porcelain often requires wet saws with diamond blades. The tradeoff is lower durability and higher porosity. Ceramic tile should always be sealed in wet areas to prevent water absorption, and it chips more easily than porcelain. For budget-conscious homeowners renovating a guest bathroom or powder room, ceramic tile delivers solid performance at a fraction of the price of natural stone.

Natural Stone Flooring

Natural stone flooring includes marble, granite, slate, travertine, and limestone. These are premium materials that bring unique visual character and timeless elegance to any space. Each slab or tile has natural variations in color, veining, and texture that cannot be replicated by manufactured products. Natural stone is the most expensive flooring option, with materials costing $6 to $14 per square foot and installation running $6 to $10 per square foot, bringing total installed costs to $12 to $22 per square foot.

Natural stone requires more maintenance than other flooring types. Most stone must be sealed upon installation and resealed every one to three years to prevent staining. Marble is particularly susceptible to etching from acidic substances like lemon juice, wine, and tomato sauce, making it a risky choice for kitchens despite its beauty. Slate and granite are harder and more forgiving for high-traffic areas. Travertine fills a middle ground with its warm, earthy tones and moderate maintenance needs. Natural stone flooring can last a lifetime with proper care, and it consistently adds significant value to a home at resale.

Carpet

Carpet remains the preferred flooring for bedrooms and bonus rooms where warmth, comfort, and sound insulation are priorities. Modern carpet fiber technology has improved stain resistance and durability considerably, with nylon and triexta (SmartStrand) fibers leading the market. Carpet materials range from $1 to $6 per square foot depending on fiber type, pile style, and density. Padding adds $0.50 to $1.50 per square foot, and installation costs $1 to $3 per square foot. Total installed cost runs $3 to $11 per square foot including pad.

The lifespan of carpet is shorter than hard-surface flooring, typically 8 to 15 years depending on quality and foot traffic. Carpet traps allergens, dust mites, and pet dander, making it a poor choice for allergy sufferers. It is also unsuitable for wet areas and should not be installed in basements prone to moisture. For homeowners on a tight budget who need to cover a large area quickly, carpet offers the lowest cost per square foot of any flooring type and provides acoustic benefits that hard surfaces cannot match.

Old Flooring Removal Costs

Before new flooring goes in, the old flooring typically needs to come out. Removal costs vary significantly based on the existing material and how it was installed.

Carpet and pad removal $0.50 — $1.50 / sq ft
Vinyl or linoleum removal $1 — $2 / sq ft
Laminate or LVP removal $0.75 — $1.50 / sq ft
Hardwood removal $2 — $4 / sq ft
Tile removal $3 — $6 / sq ft
Dumpster rental for debris $300 — $600

Tile removal is the most expensive because the thinset mortar underneath must also be chipped away and the subfloor cleaned or repaired. Vinyl flooring installed before 1986 may contain asbestos in the adhesive or backing, which requires professional abatement at $5 to $15 per square foot. Always have old vinyl tested before attempting removal if your home was built before the late 1980s.

Subfloor Preparation Costs

The condition of your subfloor directly impacts the quality and longevity of your new flooring. Most flooring materials require a clean, level, dry surface for proper installation. If your subfloor is uneven, damaged, or moisture-compromised, prep work is essential and adds to the total project cost.

Self-leveling compound (minor unevenness) $2 — $5 / sq ft
Plywood subfloor repair or replacement $3 — $8 / sq ft
Moisture barrier installation $0.50 — $1.50 / sq ft
Concrete crack repair $150 — $500 per area
Cement board underlayment (for tile) $2 — $4 / sq ft

Skipping subfloor prep to save money is one of the most common mistakes homeowners make. Uneven subfloors cause click-lock LVP and laminate planks to separate at the seams. Moisture trapped under hardwood causes cupping and warping. Tile installed on a subfloor with too much flex will crack grout lines and eventually crack the tiles themselves. Investing in proper subfloor preparation protects your new flooring investment for decades.

Trim, Molding, and Transition Costs

New flooring almost always means new trim and transition pieces. Baseboards, shoe molding, and transitions between rooms and flooring types are the finishing touches that make a flooring project look complete and professional.

Baseboard removal and reinstall $1 — $2 / linear ft
New baseboards (material + install) $3 — $8 / linear ft
Quarter round or shoe molding $1 — $3 / linear ft
Transition strips (T-molding, reducers) $5 — $20 per piece
Stair nosing (per tread) $15 — $45 per tread

For a typical 1,500-square-foot home, expect baseboard and trim costs to add $500 to $2,000 to the total flooring budget. If you are installing flooring on stairs, stair nosing and tread caps can add $40 to $100 per step depending on the material. Many homeowners forget to budget for these items and are caught off guard when the installer presents an additional bill at the end of the project.

Whole House Flooring Costs

Replacing flooring throughout an entire home is one of the most cost-effective approaches because installers offer better per-square-foot pricing on larger jobs, and you eliminate the cost of multiple transition strips between different flooring types. Here is what to expect for whole-house flooring based on home size.

1,000 sq ft home — LVP throughout $5,000 — $10,000
1,500 sq ft home — LVP throughout $7,500 — $15,000
2,000 sq ft home — LVP throughout $10,000 — $20,000
2,000 sq ft home — Engineered hardwood $14,000 — $30,000
2,000 sq ft home — Solid hardwood $18,000 — $38,000
2,500 sq ft home — Mixed (LVP + tile + carpet) $15,000 — $30,000

A common whole-house strategy is to use LVP or engineered hardwood in the main living areas and hallways, porcelain tile in bathrooms and the laundry room, and carpet in the bedrooms. This combination balances cost, durability, and comfort across different areas while maintaining a cohesive look. Most flooring contractors offer 5 to 15 percent discounts on whole-house projects compared to room-by-room pricing.

Best Flooring by Room

Not every flooring type works in every room. Moisture exposure, foot traffic, comfort needs, and temperature variation all influence which material is the best fit for each area of your home.

  • Kitchen — LVP, porcelain tile, or engineered hardwood. Kitchens need waterproof or water-resistant flooring that handles spills, dropped dishes, and heavy foot traffic. LVP is the most popular kitchen flooring in 2026 due to its waterproof properties and comfort underfoot.
  • Bathroom — Porcelain tile, ceramic tile, or LVP. Bathrooms require fully waterproof flooring. Tile remains the traditional favorite, but LVP is gaining ground rapidly because it is warmer underfoot and less slippery when wet.
  • Living room — Solid hardwood, engineered hardwood, or LVP. The living room is where aesthetics matter most. Hardwood floors create warmth and visual appeal, while LVP offers a budget-friendly alternative with similar looks.
  • Bedroom — Carpet, hardwood, or engineered hardwood. Bedrooms benefit from the softness and warmth of carpet, especially in colder climates. Hardwood with area rugs is an increasingly popular choice that balances aesthetics with comfort.
  • Basement — LVP, engineered hardwood over a moisture barrier, or porcelain tile. Basements are prone to moisture and temperature fluctuations. Solid hardwood and traditional laminate should be avoided. LVP is the safest and most cost-effective basement flooring option.
  • Entryway and mudroom — Porcelain tile or slate. These high-abuse areas need flooring that handles mud, snow, water, and heavy foot traffic without showing wear. Tile is the most practical choice and easiest to clean.
  • Laundry room — LVP or porcelain tile. Waterproof flooring is essential in case of washer leaks or hose failures. LVP is easier to install and more comfortable to stand on during folding and sorting.

Durability and Lifespan by Flooring Type

Flooring is a long-term investment, and understanding how long each material lasts helps you calculate the true cost per year of ownership. Here is how the major flooring types stack up on lifespan and maintenance requirements.

Solid Hardwood 50 — 100 years
Natural Stone 75 — 100+ years
Porcelain Tile 50 — 75 years
Ceramic Tile 40 — 60 years
Engineered Hardwood 25 — 50 years
Luxury Vinyl Plank (LVP) 15 — 25 years
Laminate 15 — 25 years
Carpet 8 — 15 years

When you factor in lifespan, solid hardwood and porcelain tile often have the lowest cost per year despite their higher upfront prices. Solid hardwood at $14 per square foot installed with a 75-year lifespan costs roughly $0.19 per square foot per year. LVP at $6 per square foot installed with a 20-year lifespan costs $0.30 per square foot per year. This long-term math is worth considering if you plan to stay in your home for decades.

DIY vs. Professional Installation

Some flooring types lend themselves well to DIY installation, while others absolutely require professional expertise. Getting this decision right can save you thousands of dollars or prevent a costly disaster.

  • Best for DIY — Click-lock LVP and laminate are the easiest flooring materials to install yourself. They float over the subfloor with no glue or nails, and most homeowners with basic tools and patience can install 200 to 300 square feet in a weekend. DIY installation saves $2 to $4 per square foot in labor costs.
  • Moderate DIY difficulty — Engineered hardwood with a click-lock system is manageable for experienced DIYers. Glue-down and nail-down engineered hardwood is more challenging and requires rental tools. Carpet tile squares are also DIY-friendly, though broadloom carpet requires professional stretching and seaming tools.
  • Hire a professional — Solid hardwood installation requires a pneumatic nailer, experience with acclimation and expansion gaps, and the skill to handle staggered patterns and cut-ins around obstacles. Tile installation demands precise leveling, proper thinset application, and expert cutting. Poor tile work leads to cracked grout, lippage, and water intrusion. Natural stone is even less forgiving due to its weight, irregular thicknesses, and the precision required during cuts.

Even for DIY-friendly materials, consider hiring a professional if your subfloor needs significant prep work, if the room has complex angles or multiple doorways, or if you are installing on stairs. The cost of fixing a botched DIY flooring job almost always exceeds what professional installation would have cost in the first place.

Moisture Considerations

Moisture is the number one enemy of many flooring types, and choosing the wrong material for a moisture-prone area is one of the most expensive flooring mistakes you can make. Understanding how each material interacts with water is essential for long-term performance.

  • Fully waterproof — Porcelain tile, ceramic tile (when sealed), LVP, and natural stone (when sealed). These materials can handle standing water, spills, and high humidity without structural damage.
  • Water-resistant — Engineered hardwood and waterproof laminate. These materials handle occasional spills and moderate humidity but will be damaged by standing water or flooding.
  • Water-sensitive — Solid hardwood, traditional laminate, and carpet. These materials should not be installed in bathrooms, basements, or laundry rooms. Even high humidity levels above 60 percent can cause solid hardwood to expand, cup, and buckle.

If you live in a humid climate or are installing flooring over a concrete slab, always perform a moisture test before installation. A calcium chloride test or relative humidity probe test costs $20 to $50 per test area and takes 24 to 72 hours. Concrete slabs that test above 75 percent relative humidity require a moisture mitigation system ($2 to $4 per square foot) before most flooring types can be installed safely.

Radiant Heat Compatibility

Radiant floor heating systems are becoming increasingly popular in 2026, especially in bathrooms, kitchens, and basements. Not all flooring materials work well over radiant heat, and choosing an incompatible material can damage both the flooring and the heating system.

  • Excellent with radiant heat — Porcelain tile, ceramic tile, and natural stone. These materials are the best conductors of heat and deliver the most efficient performance with radiant systems. Tile over radiant heat is the gold standard for heated bathroom floors.
  • Good with radiant heat — Engineered hardwood and laminate. The layered construction of these materials handles thermal expansion better than solid wood. Check manufacturer specifications for maximum surface temperature limits, typically 80 to 85 degrees Fahrenheit.
  • Acceptable with radiant heat — LVP. Most LVP manufacturers now rate their products for use over radiant heat, but maximum temperature limits are lower (typically 80 degrees Fahrenheit). Always verify the specific product's radiant heat compatibility before purchasing.
  • Not recommended — Solid hardwood and carpet. Solid wood dries out and develops gaps over radiant heat. Carpet insulates against the heat, reducing system efficiency by 20 to 40 percent and forcing the system to work harder and consume more energy.

Electric radiant heat mats add $8 to $15 per square foot installed to your flooring budget. Hydronic (water-based) radiant heat systems cost $12 to $25 per square foot installed but are more efficient for whole-house applications. Factor radiant heat costs into your total flooring budget if you are planning to add heated floors during your renovation.

Money-Saving Tips for Your Flooring Project

  • Buy 10 percent extra material — Flooring installation always produces waste from cuts and fitting. Ordering 10 percent extra up front costs far less than placing a second order for a small quantity later, and it ensures you have matching material for future repairs.
  • Shop end-of-lot and clearance inventory — Big-box stores and flooring showrooms regularly discount discontinued colors and end-of-lot inventory by 30 to 50 percent. If you are flexible on color, clearance flooring is the best deal in the business.
  • Get at least three quotes — Flooring installation pricing varies significantly between contractors in the same market. Collect three to five itemized quotes that break out material, labor, removal, prep, and trim separately so you can compare apples to apples.
  • Do the removal yourself — Ripping out old carpet, laminate, or vinyl is physically demanding but technically simple. DIY removal saves $0.50 to $4 per square foot depending on the existing material. Rent a dumpster for $300 to $500 and set aside a weekend for the work.
  • Negotiate whole-house pricing — Contractors prefer large jobs with fewer mobilizations. If you are replacing flooring in multiple rooms, negotiate a package price rather than pricing each room individually. Discounts of 5 to 15 percent are common on whole-house projects.
  • Time your purchase for sales — The best flooring deals typically happen during Black Friday, Memorial Day, and Labor Day sales events. Many retailers also run January clearance sales to make room for new product lines. Buying materials during a sale and scheduling installation for a few weeks later can save hundreds or thousands of dollars.
  • Consider LVP over hardwood for rentals — If you own rental properties or plan to sell within five years, LVP offers the best combination of durability, appearance, and cost. It withstands tenant wear better than hardwood and costs 40 to 60 percent less to install.

Red Flags When Hiring a Flooring Installer

  • Requesting more than 50 percent of the total cost upfront — reputable installers typically collect a 10 to 25 percent deposit with the balance due upon completion
  • No written estimate or contract that itemizes material, labor, removal, prep, and trim costs separately
  • Refusing to show proof of contractor's license, liability insurance, and workers' compensation coverage
  • Quoting a price without ever seeing the subfloor or measuring the space in person
  • Significantly underbidding all other quotes — this usually means substandard materials, unlicensed labor, or hidden upcharges that appear after the work begins
  • Pressuring you to skip acclimation time for hardwood — most solid and engineered hardwood must acclimate in your home for 3 to 5 days before installation to adjust to your home's humidity level
  • Refusing to warranty their installation work — most professional installers offer a 1 to 2 year labor warranty in addition to the manufacturer's material warranty
  • Suggesting they install flooring directly over damaged or unlevel subfloor to save time
