A fresh coat of exterior paint does more than improve curb appeal. It protects your siding from moisture damage, UV degradation, and insect intrusion, and it can add thousands of dollars to your home's resale value. But exterior painting is a major project with costs that vary significantly based on your home's size, condition, siding type, and the quality of paint you choose. Understanding these factors helps you budget realistically and evaluate contractor quotes with confidence.

In 2026, exterior house painting typically costs between $2,000 and $8,000 for a standard single-family home, with most homeowners paying around $3,500 to $5,500. On a per-square-foot basis, expect to pay $1.50 to $4.00 including labor and materials. Larger homes, multi-story buildings, and extensive prep work push costs toward the higher end. Below, we break down every factor that affects your final price.

Quick Cost Summary

Here is a snapshot of exterior painting costs in 2026 by home size. These figures include labor, paint, primer, and standard prep work.

1-story home, ~1,500 sq ft $2,000 – $4,000
2-story home, ~2,500 sq ft $3,500 – $7,000
3-story or large home, ~3,500+ sq ft $6,000 – $12,000+
Per square foot (labor + paint) $1.50 – $4.00
Trim, shutters, and doors only $500 – $2,500
Single accent wall or section $300 – $800

Square footage for exterior painting is measured by the paintable surface area (walls, trim, soffits), not your home's interior floor plan. A 2,000 square foot home typically has 3,000 to 4,000 square feet of paintable exterior surface when you account for wall height, gables, and trim.

Paint Quality Tiers and Pricing

The quality of paint you choose has a direct impact on both the upfront cost and how long the job lasts before needing to be redone.

Builder-grade / economy paint (per gallon) $20 – $35
Mid-range paint — Behr, Valspar (per gallon) $35 – $55
Premium paint — Benjamin Moore, Sherwin-Williams (per gallon) $50 – $85

Builder-grade paints are the cheapest option and deliver acceptable coverage for a fresh appearance. However, they contain less pigment and fewer binding resins, which means they fade faster, chalk more quickly, and need repainting in 3 to 5 years. They are best suited for situations where you are painting to sell and do not need long-term durability.

Mid-range paints from brands like Behr (sold at Home Depot) and Valspar (sold at Lowe's) offer significantly better durability, coverage, and color retention. A quality mid-range exterior paint lasts 7 to 10 years and provides excellent value for most homeowners. Many professional painters default to this tier for residential work.

Premium paints from Benjamin Moore (Regal Select, Aura), Sherwin-Williams (Duration, Emerald), and PPG offer the best durability, color retention, and coverage. Premium paints last 10 to 15 years or more and provide superior resistance to fading, cracking, and mildew. The higher per-gallon cost is offset by longer intervals between repaints, often making premium paint the cheapest option over a 20-year timeframe.

A typical 2,500 square foot home requires 15 to 25 gallons of paint for two coats, depending on siding porosity and color changes. The difference between economy and premium paint on that volume is $500 to $1,000, a meaningful but not overwhelming addition to the total project cost.

Siding Type and Its Impact on Cost

The material your home's exterior is made from affects prep time, paint adhesion, and how much paint is needed.

Wood siding (clapboard, shingle) $1.50 – $3.50 per sq ft
Vinyl siding $1.00 – $2.50 per sq ft
Stucco $1.50 – $4.00 per sq ft
Brick (painting, not limewash) $2.00 – $4.50 per sq ft
Fiber cement (HardiePlank) $1.50 – $3.00 per sq ft

Wood siding is the most common painting surface and accepts paint well when properly prepared. Older wood siding may have peeling or flaking paint that requires extensive scraping and sanding before new paint can be applied. This prep work can add significant labor time.

Stucco is porous and absorbs more paint than smooth surfaces, often requiring an extra coat. Stucco also tends to develop hairline cracks that need to be filled before painting. Using elastomeric paint, which is specifically designed for stucco and expands to bridge small cracks, adds $10 to $20 per gallon but delivers much better results and longevity.

Brick is the most expensive to paint because the rough, porous surface requires significantly more paint and labor. Brick also requires special masonry primer. Once painted, brick must be repainted periodically since peeling paint on brick is very difficult to remove. Many homeowners are choosing limewash or mineral paint as alternatives that allow the brick to breathe.

Prep Work: The Hidden Cost Driver

Experienced painters will tell you that preparation accounts for 60 to 70 percent of the total work time on an exterior painting project. Thorough prep is what separates a paint job that lasts three years from one that lasts ten.

Power washing $200 – $500
Scraping and sanding (moderate peeling) $500 – $1,500
Caulking windows, doors, and trim $200 – $600
Wood rot repair (per area) $100 – $500
Lead paint testing (pre-1978 homes) $25 – $75
Lead-safe prep work (if lead found) $500 – $3,000+ extra

Power washing removes dirt, mildew, and loose paint and is the first step in any exterior paint job. It should be done at least 24 to 48 hours before painting to allow surfaces to dry completely.

Scraping and sanding is necessary wherever existing paint is peeling, bubbling, or flaking. All loose paint must be removed and the edges feathered smooth before new paint is applied. On severely deteriorated surfaces, this step alone can take a full day or more.

Lead paint is a concern for any home built before 1978. Federal EPA regulations require that painters follow lead-safe work practices (RRP rule) when disturbing lead-based paint. This includes containment, specialized cleanup, and documentation. Lead-safe prep adds significant cost to the project but is legally required and protects your family's health.

Labor Costs

Most professional exterior painters charge by the square foot or provide a flat bid for the entire project rather than charging hourly. However, understanding hourly rates helps you evaluate whether a flat bid is fair.

Professional painter labor rate $25 – $75 per hour
Typical crew size 2 – 4 painters
Average project duration 3 – 7 days

A typical 2-story home takes a crew of three painters approximately four to five working days, including prep, priming, and two coats of paint. This translates to roughly 100 to 150 labor hours, which at $35 to $50 per hour per painter, accounts for the bulk of the project cost.

DIY vs. Professional Exterior Painting

Exterior painting is one of the DIY projects where the savings are significant but so are the risks.

DIY saves 60 to 70 percent of the total cost since you eliminate labor, which is the biggest expense. For a 2,500 square foot home, DIY materials (paint, primer, brushes, rollers, tape, drop cloths) cost approximately $800 to $1,500 compared to $3,500 to $7,000 for a professional job.

However, exterior painting requires working on ladders or scaffolding at height, which presents real safety risks. It also requires significant time, typically two to four weekends for a single homeowner or a full week of vacation time. The biggest risk is not achieving proper surface preparation, which causes the new paint to peel within one to three years, requiring you to redo the entire project.

DIY is most practical for single-story homes in good condition where minimal prep work is needed. For two-story or taller homes, homes requiring extensive prep, or any situation involving lead paint, hiring professionals is the smarter choice.

How Long Does Exterior Paint Last?

The longevity of an exterior paint job depends on paint quality, surface preparation, climate, and the orientation of each wall.

South and west-facing walls receive the most sun exposure and typically show fading and wear first. North-facing walls retain their color longest but may develop mildew in shaded, moist conditions. Quality paint on well-prepared surfaces lasts 7 to 12 years on average, with premium paints exceeding 10 to 15 years in moderate climates.

Climate extremes shorten paint life. Homes in desert climates contend with intense UV exposure, while coastal homes face salt spray. Northern homes undergo freeze-thaw cycles that stress paint films. If you live in a harsh climate, investing in premium paint pays for itself through extended intervals between repaints. Red Flags When Hiring Painters

The painting industry has a wide range of quality levels. Watch for these warning signs.

No written estimate or contract. Professional painters provide detailed written proposals that specify the scope of work, prep included, number of coats, paint brand and product, timeline, and warranty. Verbal agreements offer no protection.

Demanding large upfront payments. A reasonable deposit is 10 to 30 percent of the total. Any painter requesting 50 percent or more upfront before starting work is a risk. Structure payments in stages tied to project milestones.

Planning only one coat. Exterior painting requires a minimum of two coats over primer for proper coverage and durability. A single coat will look acceptable initially but will fade and fail much sooner. If a low bid assumes one coat, it is not a fair comparison to two-coat bids.

Skipping prep work. If a painter's bid does not include power washing, scraping, sanding, and caulking, the paint will not adhere properly regardless of quality. Low bids often cut prep to lower the price, which results in a paint job that fails prematurely.

Money-Saving Tips for Exterior Painting

A few smart decisions can significantly reduce your exterior painting costs without compromising quality.

Paint before the surface deteriorates badly. Repainting while the existing paint is still in fair condition eliminates hours of scraping and sanding, which reduces labor costs by 20 to 30 percent. Waiting until paint is peeling extensively makes every aspect of the job more expensive.

Choose one body color. Each additional color adds setup time, masking, and cleanup. A single body color with contrasting trim is the most cost-effective approach and keeps the home looking clean and cohesive.

Schedule in late fall or early spring. Painters are busiest in summer. Booking during shoulder season can yield 10 to 20 percent discounts and faster scheduling. Just ensure temperatures will remain above 50 degrees for proper paint curing.

Do your own prep. If you are comfortable with power washing, light scraping, and caulking, handling the prep yourself and hiring painters for priming and painting only can save $500 to $1,500. Frequently Asked Questions

What time of year is best for exterior painting? Late spring through early fall is ideal in most climates. Paint needs temperatures above 50 degrees Fahrenheit and low humidity to cure properly. Avoid painting in direct hot sun (above 90 degrees), during rain, or when overnight temperatures drop below 50 degrees within 24 hours of application.

How many coats of exterior paint do I need? Two coats over primer is the standard for a professional job. If you are painting over a similar color in good condition, one coat of premium paint may suffice for a touch-up, but for a full repaint or color change, two coats are essential for complete coverage and maximum durability.

Should I paint or replace my siding? If your siding is structurally sound with no rot or major damage, painting is dramatically cheaper and extends the siding's life by 7 to 15 years. Siding replacement costs $8,000 to $20,000 or more, making painting the better value in most cases. If more than 20 to 30 percent of the siding is rotted or damaged, replacement may be the more cost-effective long-term choice.

Do painters move furniture and plants? Professional painters handle moving lightweight items like patio furniture and potted plants away from the house. However, heavy planters, hot tubs, and built-in features are your responsibility. Discuss access and clearance requirements before the project starts so there are no surprises on painting day.
