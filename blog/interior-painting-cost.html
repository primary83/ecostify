How Much Does Interior Painting Cost in 2026?

Updated February 2026 · 11 min read

A fresh coat of paint is one of the fastest, most affordable ways to transform the look and feel of your home. Whether you are refreshing a single bedroom or repainting every room before listing your house for sale, understanding what interior painting costs in 2026 will help you budget accurately, compare contractor quotes, and decide whether to DIY or hire a pro. This guide covers every cost variable from paint quality and finish types to labor rates, prep work, and the price of painting trim, ceilings, and accent walls so you can plan your project with confidence.

Quick Cost Summary

Interior painting costs vary depending on room size, ceiling height, paint quality, surface condition, and whether you hire a professional or do it yourself. Here is a snapshot of what homeowners are paying nationally in 2026.

Cost per square foot (walls only) $2 — $6
Average room (12 x 12, walls + ceiling) $300 — $800
Whole house (1,500 sq ft interior) $3,500 — $8,000
Whole house (2,500 sq ft interior) $5,500 — $13,000
DIY cost per room (paint + supplies) $100 — $300

The national average for a professionally painted room in 2026 sits around $400 to $600. Most homeowners spend between $3,500 and $8,000 to paint the entire interior of an average-sized home when hiring a professional crew. Your final number will depend heavily on how much prep work the walls need, the quality of paint you choose, ceiling height, and your local labor market.

Cost Per Room

Different rooms come with different price tags based on their size, the number of windows and doors that need to be cut around, and the complexity of the surfaces. Here is what you can expect to pay per room when hiring a professional painter in 2026.

Bedroom

Small bedroom (10 x 10) $250 — $550
Standard bedroom (12 x 12) $350 — $700
Master bedroom (14 x 16 or larger) $500 — $1,000

Bedrooms are typically the most straightforward rooms to paint because they have flat, uninterrupted walls and standard 8-foot ceilings. A master bedroom costs more simply because of the added square footage and often includes a walk-in closet that needs painting as well. If you have vaulted or cathedral ceilings, expect to pay 20 to 40 percent more due to the ladder work, scaffolding, and added time required to reach high walls safely.

Living Room

Standard living room (15 x 18) $500 — $1,000
Great room / open concept (20 x 25+) $900 — $1,800

Living rooms and family rooms tend to be the largest spaces in the house, which drives up both paint quantity and labor hours. Open-concept floor plans where the living room flows into the kitchen and dining area are especially common in newer homes. Painting these open areas requires careful coordination to ensure color transitions look clean and consistent, particularly when different rooms share a continuous wall line without a natural break.

Bathroom

Half bath / powder room $150 — $350
Full bathroom $250 — $500

Bathrooms are small, but they cost more per square foot than bedrooms because of the extra prep work involved. High humidity means painters need to use moisture-resistant paint (typically a satin or semi-gloss finish), and the tight quarters around vanities, toilets, and tile surrounds slow down the cutting-in process. If you are painting as part of a larger bathroom renovation, our bathroom remodel cost guide covers every component from tile to plumbing.

Kitchen

Small to mid-size kitchen (walls only) $300 — $600
Large kitchen with high ceilings $500 — $1,000

Kitchens involve more cutting in around cabinets, backsplashes, and appliances than any other room. The actual paintable wall area is often smaller than you might expect because upper and lower cabinets cover a significant portion of the walls, but the labor per square foot is higher because of the precision required. Grease buildup near the stove also requires thorough cleaning and possibly a coat of stain-blocking primer before new paint will adhere properly. Cost Per Square Foot

When contractors quote interior painting projects, they typically price by the square foot of paintable wall surface or by the room. Understanding the per-square-foot range helps you sanity-check any quote you receive.

Walls only (standard prep) $2 — $4 / sq ft
Walls only (extensive prep needed) $3 — $6 / sq ft
Walls + ceiling $3 — $6 / sq ft
Walls + ceiling + trim $4 — $7 / sq ft

Keep in mind that square footage in painting quotes refers to the paintable wall surface area, not the floor area. A 12 x 12 room with 8-foot ceilings has roughly 384 square feet of wall space (minus windows and doors). To quickly estimate your wall square footage, add up the length of all walls, multiply by the ceiling height, then subtract about 20 square feet for each standard window and 21 square feet for each door.

Paint Quality Tiers

The quality of paint you choose has a significant impact on both the upfront cost and the longevity of your paint job. In 2026, paint falls into three general tiers, each with a different price point and performance profile.

Economy Tier ($20 to $35 per gallon)

Economy paints from store brands and budget lines like Glidden, ColorPlace, and certain Behr lines offer acceptable coverage at the lowest price point. These paints typically require two to three coats for full coverage, have lower scrub resistance, and may fade or show wear marks sooner than premium options. Economy paint makes sense for rental properties, rooms you plan to repaint soon, or large projects where budget is the primary constraint. One gallon covers roughly 350 to 400 square feet per coat.

Mid-Range Tier ($35 to $55 per gallon)

Mid-range paints from brands like Behr Marquee, Sherwin-Williams Harmony, and Benjamin Moore ben deliver noticeably better coverage, typically in one to two coats. They offer improved scrub resistance, better color retention over time, and smoother application with fewer brush and roller marks. For most homeowners painting their primary residence, this tier provides the best balance of price and performance. The improved coverage also reduces labor time, which can offset the higher per-gallon cost when hiring a professional.

Premium Tier ($55 to $90+ per gallon)

Premium paints like Benjamin Moore Regal Select and Aura, Sherwin-Williams Emerald and Duration, and Farrow and Ball estate emulsion represent the top of the market. These paints offer exceptional one-coat coverage on most colors, superior stain and scrub resistance, excellent color depth and richness, and the longest-lasting finish. Premium paints are worth the investment for high-traffic areas like hallways, kitchens, and kids' rooms, as well as for deep or saturated colors that are difficult to achieve with cheaper formulations. Benjamin Moore Aura and Sherwin-Williams Emerald are the two most popular premium lines in 2026 and are widely considered industry benchmarks for quality.

Economy paint (per gallon) $20 — $35
Mid-range paint (per gallon) $35 — $55
Premium paint (per gallon) $55 — $90+
Primer (per gallon) $20 — $45
Specialty primer — stain blocking (per gallon) $30 — $55

Prep Work Costs

Preparation is where professional painters earn their money. A quality paint job is only as good as the surface beneath it, and cutting corners on prep is the number-one reason paint jobs fail prematurely. The amount of prep required directly affects the total project cost.

Light prep (clean, tape, minor touch-ups) $0.50 — $1.00 / sq ft
Moderate prep (patching holes, sanding, caulking) $1.00 — $2.00 / sq ft
Heavy prep (skim coating, wallpaper removal, major repairs) $2.00 — $4.00 / sq ft
Wallpaper removal (per room) $200 — $800
Lead paint testing and abatement $300 — $2,500+

Standard prep for walls in good condition includes filling nail holes and small dents with spackle, lightly sanding patched areas, caulking gaps between trim and walls, and laying down drop cloths and painter's tape. Walls with extensive damage, old wallpaper, textured coatings, or heavy staining require more intensive preparation that can double or triple the prep portion of the estimate. If your home was built before 1978, lead paint testing is required by federal law before any surface disturbance. Lead paint abatement by a certified contractor adds significant cost but is non-negotiable for health and safety.

Trim, Ceiling, and Accent Wall Costs

Most homeowners focus on wall color when planning a painting project, but trim, ceilings, and accent walls are where the final result goes from good to great. Here is what these additions cost.

Trim and Baseboards

Baseboard painting (per linear foot) $1 — $3
Crown molding (per linear foot) $1 — $3
Door frame and casing (per door) $25 — $75
Window trim (per window) $25 — $60
Interior door — both sides (per door) $75 — $175

Painting trim is slow, detail-oriented work that requires a steady hand and high-quality brushes. Trim is almost always painted with a semi-gloss or satin finish for durability and easy cleaning, which highlights any imperfections in the surface. Properly prepping trim, which usually means sanding, filling nail holes, and caulking, takes as long as the actual painting. For a typical three-bedroom house with eight doors and twelve windows, expect to spend $800 to $2,500 for trim painting if hired out professionally.

Ceiling Painting

Standard flat ceiling (per sq ft) $1 — $2.50
Vaulted or cathedral ceiling (per sq ft) $2 — $4
Textured ceiling (popcorn, knockdown) $1.50 — $3

Ceiling painting is often an afterthought, but a freshly painted ceiling makes a dramatic difference, especially when the existing ceiling has yellowed from age, cooking grease, or cigarette smoke. Most ceiling paint is a flat white, which hides imperfections and reflects light evenly across the room. Vaulted and cathedral ceilings cost more because they require extension poles, scaffolding, or ladder work that slows the pace significantly. If you have old popcorn ceilings and want them removed before painting, budget an additional $1 to $3 per square foot for scraping, skim coating, and sanding before any paint goes on.

Accent Walls

Single accent wall — solid color $100 — $300
Accent wall — geometric pattern or stripes $200 — $600
Accent wall — faux finish or specialty technique $400 — $1,200

Accent walls are one of the most popular interior painting trends in 2026. A single wall painted in a bold, contrasting color creates a focal point in any room at minimal cost. More complex accent wall treatments like geometric tape patterns, color blocking, ombre effects, or faux finishes like limewash and Venetian plaster require specialized skill and additional time. Dark and saturated accent colors may also need three or more coats for full, even coverage, particularly over light-colored existing paint.

Whole-House Interior Painting Costs

Painting the entire interior of your home at once is the most cost-effective approach because painters can work efficiently from room to room, bulk-buy paint at a discount, and set up equipment once. Here is what whole-house painting typically costs in 2026 based on home size.

1,000 sq ft home (walls only) $2,500 — $5,500
1,500 sq ft home (walls only) $3,500 — $8,000
2,000 sq ft home (walls only) $4,500 — $10,000
2,500 sq ft home (walls only) $5,500 — $13,000
3,000+ sq ft home (walls only) $7,000 — $16,000+

These ranges assume standard 8-foot ceilings, walls in fair to good condition, and two coats of mid-range paint. Add 15 to 25 percent for ceilings, and another 15 to 20 percent for all trim, baseboards, and doors. A full-service whole-house repaint including walls, ceilings, and all trim for a 2,000-square-foot home typically comes in between $7,000 and $15,000 with a professional crew using quality paint.

Labor Costs

Labor typically accounts for 70 to 85 percent of a professional interior painting quote. Paint and materials are relatively inexpensive compared to the time it takes to properly prepare, prime, cut in, and roll every surface. Here is what painters charge in 2026.

Professional painter (per hour) $35 — $70
Painting crew (2-3 painters, per hour) $80 — $180
Specialty faux finisher (per hour) $60 — $100
Typical labor as % of total project 70% — 85%

Labor rates vary significantly by region. Painters in major metro areas like New York, San Francisco, Seattle, and Boston charge 30 to 50 percent more than the national average, while painters in smaller cities and rural areas are often 10 to 20 percent below. The experience level of the painter also matters. A seasoned professional who can cut a razor-sharp line between wall and ceiling without tape works faster and produces a cleaner result than an entry-level painter, and their hourly rate reflects that skill. Always get at least three written quotes from licensed and insured painters to understand the going rate in your specific market.

DIY vs. Professional Painting

Interior painting is one of the most popular DIY home improvement projects, and for good reason. The tools are affordable, the skills are learnable, and the savings compared to hiring a pro are substantial. But there are real tradeoffs to consider.

DIY Painting Costs

Paint (2 gallons per room, mid-range) $70 — $110
Rollers, brushes, trays, extension pole $30 — $60
Painter's tape (3-4 rolls) $15 — $25
Drop cloths, spackle, sandpaper, caulk $20 — $40
Total DIY cost per room $100 — $300

DIY makes sense when: you are painting one to three rooms with standard ceilings, the walls are in good condition and need minimal prep, you are comfortable on a ladder and have a full weekend to dedicate to the project, and you enjoy the hands-on satisfaction of doing it yourself. A homeowner who paints a single bedroom will spend $100 to $250 on materials versus $350 to $700 for a professional, saving roughly 50 to 70 percent.

Hire a professional when: you are painting the entire house, rooms have high or vaulted ceilings, walls need extensive repair or skim coating, you want flawless trim and ceiling lines, the home has lead paint that requires certified handling, or your time is worth more than the labor savings. A professional two-person crew can paint a full room in three to five hours. The same room might take an inexperienced homeowner an entire weekend once you factor in taping, cutting in, rolling, and waiting for coats to dry.

How to Estimate Paint Needed

Buying the right amount of paint avoids both wasteful overbuying and the frustrating color-match problems that come from needing an extra gallon mid-project. Here is a simple formula for estimating paint quantity.

Step 1: Measure the total length of all walls in the room and multiply by the ceiling height to get the gross wall area. For a 12 x 12 room with 8-foot ceilings, that is (12 + 12 + 12 + 12) x 8 = 384 square feet.

Step 2: Subtract openings. Deduct roughly 20 square feet for each standard window and 21 square feet for each door. A room with two windows and one door would subtract about 61 square feet, leaving 323 square feet of paintable wall.

Step 3: Divide by coverage rate. One gallon of paint covers approximately 350 to 400 square feet per coat. For two coats on 323 square feet, you need about 646 square feet of coverage, which works out to roughly 1.6 to 1.8 gallons. Round up and buy two gallons.

Pro tip: Always buy 10 to 15 percent more paint than your calculation suggests. Having leftover paint for future touch-ups is far better than running short and hoping the store can mix an exact match from a different batch. Store leftover paint in a cool, dry place with the lid tightly sealed, and label the can with the room name and date.

Paint Finish Types

The sheen level you choose affects the appearance, durability, and cleanability of the finished surface. Each finish has a specific use case, and choosing the wrong one is a common mistake that leads to disappointing results.

Flat / Matte Best for ceilings, low-traffic rooms
Eggshell Best for bedrooms, living rooms, dining rooms
Satin Best for kitchens, bathrooms, hallways, kids' rooms
Semi-Gloss Best for trim, doors, baseboards, cabinets

Flat and matte finishes hide surface imperfections better than any other sheen because they do not reflect light. They create a smooth, velvety look that works beautifully on ceilings and in formal living rooms or bedrooms with minimal traffic. The downside is poor washability. Scrubbing a stain off a flat-painted wall often removes the paint along with the stain, making flat a poor choice for hallways, kids' rooms, or kitchens.

Eggshell is the most popular finish for general interior walls in 2026. It has a very subtle sheen, just enough to improve washability without the noticeable shine of a satin or semi-gloss. Eggshell is forgiving on walls with minor imperfections and holds up to light cleaning with a damp cloth. It is an excellent all-purpose choice for bedrooms, living rooms, and dining rooms.

Satin has a soft, pearl-like sheen that is noticeably more reflective than eggshell. It offers excellent stain resistance and scrub durability, making it the preferred choice for high-traffic areas, kids' rooms, bathrooms, kitchens, and mudrooms. The tradeoff is that satin shows surface imperfections more readily than eggshell or flat, so walls need to be well-prepped before applying a satin finish.

Semi-gloss is the standard finish for trim, baseboards, doors, and window casings. Its higher sheen creates a crisp, clean look that highlights architectural detail and is very easy to wipe clean. Semi-gloss is also commonly used in bathrooms and kitchens where moisture resistance is important. It is rarely used on large wall areas because the high reflectivity can feel harsh and accentuates every bump and dent in the surface.

Project Timeline

Understanding how long your painting project will take helps you plan around the disruption and set realistic expectations with your painter. Here is what to expect for common project scopes.

Single room (walls only, good condition) 3 — 5 hours
Single room (walls, ceiling, trim) 1 — 1.5 days
Three to four rooms 2 — 4 days
Whole house — 1,500 sq ft (walls only) 3 — 5 days
Whole house — 2,500 sq ft (walls, ceilings, trim) 7 — 12 days

These timelines assume a two to three person crew, standard ceilings, and walls in fair to good condition. Projects requiring heavy prep, wallpaper removal, or extensive patching can take 30 to 50 percent longer. Dry time between coats is typically two to four hours for latex paint, so professional crews optimize their workflow by cutting in one room while rolling another, then returning for second coats once the first has dried. Weather and humidity also affect dry times. High humidity slows drying and can cause paint to sag or not cure properly, which is why professional painters prefer working in climate-controlled interior conditions.

Tips to Save Money on Interior Painting

  • Paint during the off-season — Painters are busiest from April through October when exterior work dominates their schedules. Booking interior painting during the winter months often gets you faster scheduling and potentially lower rates since contractors are looking to fill their calendar.
  • Choose fewer colors — Every additional color change requires separate mixing, extra cutting in, and more brush cleanup time. Painting an entire house in two or three complementary colors is significantly cheaper than choosing a different color for every room.
  • Do your own prep — Moving furniture to the center of the room, removing outlet covers and switch plates, filling nail holes with spackle, and laying down drop cloths yourself can save $200 to $500 on a whole-house project by reducing the painter's prep time.
  • Buy paint on sale — Both Sherwin-Williams and Benjamin Moore run major sales multiple times per year, often offering 30 to 40 percent off retail prices. Timing your paint purchase to coincide with a sale can save hundreds of dollars on a large project.
  • Use a paint-and-primer-in-one — Modern paint-and-primer formulations from premium brands eliminate the need for a separate primer coat on walls that are in good condition and not changing dramatically in color. This reduces both material and labor costs.
  • Bundle rooms together — Most painters offer per-room discounts when you hire them for multiple rooms or a whole-house project. The setup and teardown overhead is spread across more square footage, which lowers the effective per-room cost.
  • Skip the accent wall complexity — A solid-color accent wall costs $100 to $300. A geometric pattern or specialty technique on the same wall costs $400 to $1,200. If budget is tight, a bold solid color delivers most of the visual impact at a fraction of the cost.
  • Provide your own paint — Some painters charge a markup on paint, typically 10 to 20 percent. Buying paint yourself and having it on-site before the crew arrives eliminates this markup. Just confirm the brand and finish with your painter first to ensure compatibility with their application process.

Red Flags When Hiring a Painter

Hiring the wrong painting contractor can result in a sloppy finish, paint on your floors and trim, and a project that takes far longer than promised. Watch out for these warning signs.

  • No written estimate or vague line items — A professional painter should provide a detailed written estimate that specifies the number of coats, the paint brand and finish, which surfaces will be painted, what prep is included, and the total price. A one-line quote that just says "paint interior — $3,000" gives you no recourse if the result is subpar.
  • No proof of insurance — Any painter working in your home should carry general liability insurance at a minimum. If a painter damages your floors, furniture, or fixtures, liability insurance covers the repair. If they are injured on the job and lack workers' compensation coverage, you could be held liable.
  • Demands full payment upfront — A reasonable payment structure is no more than 25 to 30 percent upfront (to cover paint and supplies), with the balance due upon satisfactory completion. A painter who demands 50 percent or more before starting has no financial incentive to finish the job or address quality issues.
  • Extremely low bid — If one bid comes in 40 percent below the other two, the painter is almost certainly cutting corners. Common shortcuts include using cheap builder-grade paint, applying only one coat, skipping primer where it is needed, minimal taping and masking, and rushing through prep work.
  • No references or portfolio — Experienced painters should have photos of recent projects and references you can call. In 2026, most reputable painters also have Google reviews or a social media presence showing their work. A complete lack of verifiable past work is a red flag.
  • Will not specify paint brand — If a painter is evasive about what paint they plan to use, they may be planning to substitute a cheaper product than what you discussed. Insist on a specific brand, product line, and finish in the written estimate.
  • Skips the walk-through — Any painter who quotes a job over the phone or via email without visiting your home to inspect wall conditions, measure the space, and discuss your expectations is guessing at the price. That guess usually leads to change orders or cut corners once work begins.
