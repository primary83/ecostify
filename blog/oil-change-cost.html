How Much Does an Oil Change Cost in 2026?

An oil change is the single most common maintenance task for any vehicle on the road, and it is also one of the most straightforward to budget for. In 2026, most drivers will pay somewhere between $35 and $125 for a standard oil change at a shop, though the final number depends on the type of oil, the service provider, and the vehicle itself. Luxury and European models can push the bill north of $150.

This guide breaks down every variable that affects pricing so you can walk into any shop knowing exactly what to expect. We cover costs by oil type, costs at every major chain, the DIY route, recommended intervals, and the money-saving strategies that actually work.

Quick Cost Summary

Here is the big picture. These ranges reflect national averages in early 2026 and include the oil filter, labor, and a standard volume of oil (typically 5 quarts).

Conventional oil change$35 – $55
Synthetic blend oil change$50 – $75
Full synthetic oil change$65 – $125
High-mileage oil change$55 – $90
European / luxury vehicle$95 – $175
DIY oil change (parts only)$25 – $60

If your vehicle requires more than 5 quarts, expect an additional $8 to $12 per extra quart. Trucks with 8-quart capacities and diesel engines that take 10+ quarts will always cost more than the baseline ranges above.

Conventional vs. Synthetic Blend vs. Full Synthetic

The type of oil going into your engine is the biggest cost driver. Understanding the differences will help you avoid paying for something you do not need, and avoid skimping on something you do.

Conventional Oil

Conventional motor oil is refined directly from crude oil. It works perfectly well in older engines with simple designs and moderate operating temperatures. If your owner's manual says conventional oil is acceptable and you drive under normal conditions, there is no mechanical reason to upgrade. Expect to pay $35 to $55 at most shops. The tradeoff is that conventional oil breaks down faster, so you will need changes more frequently, typically every 3,000 to 5,000 miles.

Synthetic Blend

A synthetic blend mixes conventional base oil with synthetic base stock. The result is better high-temperature stability and slightly longer service life than pure conventional oil, without the full cost of a synthetic change. This is a solid middle-ground option for trucks, SUVs, and vehicles that tow or haul regularly. Pricing sits at $50 to $75 in 2026.

Full Synthetic

Full synthetic oil is engineered molecule by molecule for maximum performance. It resists thermal breakdown, flows better in extreme cold, and lasts significantly longer between changes. Most vehicles manufactured after 2015 either require or strongly recommend full synthetic oil. Prices run from $65 to $125, but you offset some of that cost by going 7,500 to 10,000 miles between changes instead of 3,000 to 5,000.

Bottom line: always check your owner's manual. If it says full synthetic, use full synthetic. If it says conventional is fine, you can save money without guilt.

Oil Change Costs by Provider

Where you get the work done matters almost as much as the oil type. Here is what the major chains and service categories are charging in 2026.

Jiffy Lube

Conventional$44 – $56
Synthetic blend$62 – $78
Full synthetic$78 – $110

Jiffy Lube remains one of the most widely available quick-lube chains with over 2,000 locations. Pricing varies by region, and they frequently offer coupons through their app and website. Their Signature Service oil change includes a multi-point inspection, tire pressure check, and fluid top-offs. Watch out for upsells on cabin air filters and fuel system treatments, which are often marked up significantly.

Valvoline Instant Oil Change

Conventional$42 – $54
Synthetic blend$60 – $76
Full synthetic$80 – $115

Valvoline runs a stay-in-your-car model, meaning you never have to sit in a waiting room. Technicians work from a pit beneath your vehicle, and the entire process usually takes 15 to 20 minutes. They use their own Valvoline-branded oils, which are well-regarded in the industry. Their app-based coupons are worth checking before every visit.

Take 5 Oil Change

Conventional$38 – $50
Synthetic blend$55 – $70
Full synthetic$72 – $105

Take 5 has expanded rapidly across the southern and midwestern United States and now operates over 900 locations. They also use a drive-through, stay-in-your-car model. Their pricing tends to be slightly lower than Jiffy Lube and Valvoline, and they run frequent buy-one-get-one or percentage-off promotions for first-time customers.

Walmart Auto Care Center

Pit Crew (conventional)$30 – $38
Standard (synthetic blend)$45 – $55
Full synthetic$55 – $80

Walmart consistently offers the lowest oil change prices in the country. The catch is availability and wait times. Not every Walmart has an auto care center, appointments fill up fast, and walk-in waits can stretch past an hour during weekends. But if budget is your priority and you can plan ahead, Walmart is hard to beat on price alone.

Dealership Service Centers

Conventional$55 – $75
Synthetic blend$70 – $95
Full synthetic$85 – $135

Dealerships charge a premium, but they use OEM-spec oil and filters, and the work is done by technicians trained specifically on your vehicle make. Many dealerships also include a complimentary multi-point inspection, car wash, and tire rotation with the service. If your vehicle is still under warranty or you want a paper trail of factory-spec maintenance, the dealership is worth considering. Keep an eye on service mailers, as dealerships often mail competitive oil change coupons to attract repeat visits.

Independent Mechanics

Conventional$35 – $55
Synthetic blend$50 – $70
Full synthetic$65 – $110

A trusted independent mechanic often delivers the best balance of price and quality. Overhead costs are lower than chain shops and dealerships, which translates to lower labor rates for you. The key is finding a reputable shop. Ask neighbors, check Google reviews, and look for ASE certification. Once you find a good one, you will likely stick with them for years.

DIY Oil Change: Is It Worth It?

Changing your own oil is one of the most accessible entry points into DIY car maintenance. The job requires minimal tools and about 30 minutes of your time once you know the process.

What You Need

5 quarts full synthetic oil$22 – $38
Oil filter$5 – $15
Drain plug washer$1 – $3
Oil drain pan (one-time)$8 – $15
Socket wrench set (one-time)$15 – $30
Jack and jack stands (one-time)$40 – $80

After the one-time tool investment, each subsequent oil change costs roughly $28 to $56 in supplies, which puts you well below what any shop charges for full synthetic service. Over the course of a year with two oil changes, you could save $80 to $140 compared to chain shop pricing.

The savings are real, but there are trade-offs. You need a flat, safe workspace. You are responsible for proper disposal of the used oil (most auto parts stores accept it for free). And you miss out on the multi-point inspection that shops typically include. If you do go the DIY route, pair it with a periodic professional inspection so nothing gets overlooked. Regular maintenance is also a great time to evaluate whether you need other services like a brake pad replacement or to check your transmission fluid condition.

Oil Change Intervals: How Often Do You Really Need One?

The old 3,000-mile rule is outdated for most modern vehicles. Here is what actually applies in 2026:

Conventional oilEvery 3,000 – 5,000 miles
Synthetic blendEvery 5,000 – 7,500 miles
Full syntheticEvery 7,500 – 10,000 miles
Extended-life syntheticEvery 10,000 – 15,000 miles

Your owner's manual is the final authority on interval recommendations. Many newer vehicles also have an oil life monitoring system that calculates the optimal change interval based on your actual driving conditions, including temperature, engine load, trip length, and RPM patterns. When that monitor says it is time, get it done.

Severe driving conditions shorten these intervals. If you regularly drive in stop-and-go traffic, extreme heat or cold, dusty environments, or tow heavy loads, reduce your interval by about 25 to 30 percent. A car rated for 10,000-mile intervals under normal conditions might need changes at 7,000 miles under severe use.

High-Mileage Oil: Do You Need It?

High-mileage motor oil is formulated for engines with 75,000 miles or more on the odometer. It contains seal conditioners that help rejuvenate aging gaskets and seals, reducing the slow oil seepage that is common in older engines. It also includes additives to reduce oil burn-off and maintain viscosity as the oil ages.

High-mileage conventional$48 – $65
High-mileage synthetic blend$58 – $80
High-mileage full synthetic$75 – $100

Is it worth the extra $10 to $15 per change? If you notice oil spots in your driveway, your engine is burning through oil between changes, or you plan to keep the vehicle past 150,000 miles, high-mileage oil is a smart investment. It is far cheaper than replacing valve cover gaskets or rear main seals. If your high-mileage vehicle has no leaks and runs fine, standard oil of the correct weight is perfectly acceptable.

European and Luxury Vehicle Oil Changes

Owners of BMW, Mercedes-Benz, Audi, Porsche, Volkswagen, and other European makes should expect to pay meaningfully more for oil changes. There are several reasons for this.

BMW (dealer)$110 – $165
Mercedes-Benz (dealer)$120 – $175
Audi / VW (dealer)$100 – $155
Porsche (dealer)$130 – $200
European vehicle (independent shop)$85 – $140

European engines typically require manufacturer-specific oil certifications such as BMW LL-01, Mercedes 229.5, or VW 502/505. These certified oils cost more per quart. Many European engines also have larger oil capacities (7 to 9 quarts is common), and their oil filters tend to be cartridge-style units that cost $12 to $25 versus $5 to $10 for a standard spin-on filter.

An independent shop that specializes in European vehicles will typically charge 25 to 40 percent less than the dealer while still using the correct certified oil. This is where you get the most value if you drive a European car. Skipping proper oil specs to save money is a false economy that can lead to accelerated engine wear and expensive drivetrain repairs down the line.

Money-Saving Tips

You do not need to overpay for oil changes. Here are the strategies that actually move the needle.

Use coupons and app deals. Jiffy Lube, Valvoline, and Take 5 all publish regular coupons through their mobile apps and websites. Typical savings range from $7 to $15 off per visit. Dealerships mail out oil change specials as well, sometimes pricing them competitively with quick-lube chains to get you in the door.

Buy oil and a filter on sale, then bring them to a shop. Some independent shops will install customer-supplied parts and only charge a labor fee, usually $20 to $30. Pair this with a buy-one-get-one oil sale at an auto parts store and you can get a full synthetic change done for under $50 total.

Use the correct oil type for your vehicle. If your manual says conventional oil is fine, do not let a technician talk you into full synthetic unless you have a specific reason to upgrade. That upsell costs you $30 to $50 extra per visit with no meaningful benefit for your engine.

Bundle services. If you also need a tire rotation, wiper blades, or an air filter, doing them all at once can reduce the total cost versus separate visits. Many shops offer package deals that bundle an oil change with a tire rotation for $10 to $20 less than the individual prices. While you are at it, consider whether protective services like ceramic coating or window tinting could save you money on long-term paint and interior maintenance.

Stick to your actual interval. If your vehicle is rated for 10,000-mile oil change intervals with full synthetic, do not change it at 5,000 miles. You are literally throwing money away. Trust the oil life monitor or the owner's manual, whichever applies to your vehicle.

Consider DIY for routine changes. As outlined above, doing it yourself saves $40 to $80 per change once you have the basic tools. Even if you only do it twice a year, the annual savings add up quickly.

Red Flags: When a Shop Is Overcharging

Most oil change shops are honest, but some exploit customer unfamiliarity. Watch for these warning signs.

Recommending a full synthetic upgrade without justification. If your vehicle runs fine on conventional oil and the manual does not specify synthetic, a blanket recommendation to upgrade is often an upsell, not a necessity. Ask them to point to the specification in your manual.

Quoting significantly above market rates. If a shop quotes $90 for a conventional oil change or $160 for a full synthetic on a standard domestic sedan, that is well above normal. Get a second quote.

Pushing unnecessary add-on services. Fuel system cleaners, engine flushes, transmission fluid flushes, and coolant flushes are real services, but they are not needed at every oil change. If a technician recommends them, ask for the mileage-based interval from your owner's manual. A good shop will respect that question.

Claiming your oil is dangerously dirty when it is not due for a change. Oil darkens as it works. Dark oil is not necessarily bad oil. If you are within your recommended interval and a technician shows you dark oil on a dipstick as a scare tactic, that is a red flag.

Refusing to show you the old filter. A reputable shop will show you the old oil filter they removed if you ask. If they are evasive about this, question whether the filter was actually replaced.

What Happens If You Skip Oil Changes?

Delaying or skipping oil changes is one of the fastest ways to destroy an engine. Old oil loses its ability to lubricate, allowing metal-on-metal contact between moving parts. It also accumulates contaminants that form sludge, which can clog oil passages and starve critical components of lubrication.

The progression looks like this: slightly increased engine wear turns into accelerated bearing wear, which leads to low oil pressure warnings, which eventually results in catastrophic engine failure. An engine replacement costs $4,000 to $8,000+ on most vehicles. That makes a $65 oil change every 7,500 miles look like the bargain it is.

Neglecting oil changes can also void your powertrain warranty if the manufacturer determines that lack of maintenance caused the failure. Keep your receipts and maintenance records.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I switch between conventional and synthetic oil?

Yes. Modern engines can switch between oil types without issue. The myth that synthetic oil causes leaks in older engines has been debunked. Just make sure you use the correct viscosity weight (for example, 5W-30) regardless of the oil type.

Why does the same oil change cost different amounts at different locations of the same chain?

Franchise locations set their own pricing based on local labor costs, rent, and competition. A Jiffy Lube in a high-cost urban area will charge more than one in a rural town. Always check the specific location's pricing before assuming the advertised national rate applies.

Is it worth getting an oil change at the dealership?

It depends on your priorities. Dealerships are more expensive on average, but they use OEM parts, employ make-specific trained technicians, and create a documented service history that can boost resale value. If your vehicle is under warranty, dealership service ensures there are no disputes about maintenance compliance. For out-of-warranty vehicles, a trusted independent mechanic typically offers the best value.

How do I know what oil weight my vehicle needs?

Check your owner's manual or the oil cap on top of your engine, which usually has the viscosity printed on it (such as 0W-20 or 5W-30). Using the wrong viscosity can affect fuel economy, engine protection, and cold-start performance. Never guess. If you are unsure, any auto parts store can look it up by your year, make, and model.

Does oil expire if I do not drive much?

Yes. Even if you barely drive, oil degrades over time from moisture absorption and oxidation. Most manufacturers recommend changing oil at least once every 12 months regardless of mileage. If your vehicle has been sitting for an extended period, change the oil before putting serious miles on it.

Do electric vehicles need oil changes?

Pure battery electric vehicles (BEVs) do not have an internal combustion engine and do not need oil changes. Plug-in hybrids (PHEVs) and traditional hybrids still have a gasoline engine that requires regular oil changes, though intervals may be longer due to reduced engine use.

Should I get a cabin air filter replacement with every oil change?

No. Cabin air filters typically last 15,000 to 30,000 miles. Quick-lube shops often recommend replacement at every visit because the markup is substantial (the filter costs $8 to $15 at an auto parts store but $25 to $40 at a shop). Replace it on the manufacturer's schedule, not the shop's suggestion.
