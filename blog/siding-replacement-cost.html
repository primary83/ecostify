Siding Replacement Cost 2026: Vinyl, Fiber Cement & Wood

Your home's siding is its first line of defense against rain, wind, snow, UV rays, and insects. Beyond protection, siding defines your home's curb appeal and can account for up to 30 percent of its exterior visual impact. When siding deteriorates, it allows moisture to infiltrate wall cavities, leading to rot, mold, structural damage, and dramatically increased heating and cooling costs. Whether you are replacing storm-damaged sections or upgrading your entire home's exterior, understanding siding costs by material helps you make a smart investment.

In 2026, siding replacement for a typical 2,000-square-foot home costs between $5,000 and $25,000 depending on the material chosen. Vinyl siding sits at the budget-friendly end at $3 to $8 per square foot installed, while premium fiber cement and natural wood range from $6 to $15 per square foot. The national average for a complete re-side falls around $10,000 to $15,000. Below, we break down every cost factor so you can budget accurately.

Quick Cost Summary

Here is what siding replacement costs in 2026 for a typical 1,500 to 2,500 square foot home, including materials, labor, and old siding removal.

Vinyl siding (full home) $5,000 – $12,000
Fiber cement / Hardie board (full home) $10,000 – $20,000
Engineered wood (LP SmartSide) $8,000 – $18,000
Natural wood (cedar, redwood) $12,000 – $25,000+
Partial siding repair (one wall) $1,500 – $5,000

These ranges include tear-off of old siding, housewrap installation or repair, and new siding with trim. Adding insulation board beneath the siding adds $1 to $3 per square foot but significantly improves energy efficiency.

Cost by Siding Material

Each siding material offers a different balance of cost, durability, maintenance, and aesthetics. Understanding these trade-offs helps you choose the right material for your budget and priorities.

Vinyl siding remains the most popular siding material in the United States, installed on roughly 30 percent of homes. Modern vinyl has improved dramatically from the thin, faded panels of decades past. Premium vinyl siding (0.044 to 0.046 inch thickness) resists fading, withstands impacts, and comes in dozens of colors and profiles including Dutch lap, board and batten, and shake. Vinyl never needs painting, resists moisture and insects, and lasts 20 to 40 years. Its main drawbacks are susceptibility to extreme heat (warping near grills or reflective windows) and a less premium appearance compared to fiber cement or wood.

Fiber cement siding (Hardie board) has become the gold standard for homeowners who want superior durability and a premium look. James Hardie dominates this market with their HardiePlank and HardieShingle product lines. Fiber cement is made from portland cement, sand, and cellulose fibers, creating a product that resists fire, rot, termites, and extreme weather. It holds paint exceptionally well and can be factory-primed or factory-painted with a 15-year color warranty. Fiber cement lasts 40 to 50+ years but costs significantly more than vinyl and requires skilled installation.

Engineered wood siding (LP SmartSide) bridges the gap between vinyl and fiber cement in both cost and performance. Made from treated wood strands bonded with resin and coated with a zinc borate overlay, engineered wood resists rot, termites, and fungal decay while maintaining the authentic look and workability of real wood. It is lighter than fiber cement (easier and cheaper to install), holds paint well, and offers good impact resistance. LP SmartSide comes with a 50-year substrate warranty and lasts 30 to 50 years.

Natural wood siding (cedar, redwood, cypress) offers unmatched beauty and character that no manufactured product can fully replicate. Wood siding can be stained to showcase the natural grain or painted in any color. Cedar and redwood contain natural oils that resist rot and insects, though they still require regular maintenance (staining or painting every 3 to 7 years). Wood siding lasts 20 to 40+ years with proper maintenance but is the most expensive and highest-maintenance option.

Vinyl siding (installed per sq ft) $3 – $8
Fiber cement / Hardie (installed per sq ft) $6 – $13
Engineered wood (installed per sq ft) $5 – $10
Cedar wood (installed per sq ft) $7 – $15
Board and batten vinyl (installed per sq ft) $4 – $9

Labor and Installation Costs

Labor typically accounts for 40 to 60 percent of total siding cost, and the rate varies significantly by material and project complexity.

Vinyl siding installation labor $1.50 – $4.00 per sq ft
Fiber cement installation labor $3.00 – $6.00 per sq ft
Wood siding installation labor $3.00 – $7.00 per sq ft
Old siding removal and disposal $0.50 – $1.50 per sq ft
Housewrap installation $0.50 – $1.00 per sq ft

Vinyl siding installs the fastest because the panels are lightweight, interlock easily, and require minimal cutting. An experienced crew can side an average home in two to four days.

Fiber cement requires specialized tools (carbide-tipped blades, dust management), careful handling (the planks are heavy and brittle), and precise installation to maintain proper gap spacing and caulking. Installation takes four to seven days and requires experienced crews familiar with the material's requirements. Improper installation is the most common cause of fiber cement failures.

Wood siding requires skilled carpenters who can properly flash, gap, and fasten each board for long-term performance. Installation takes five to eight days depending on the profile (lap siding is faster than shingles or board and batten).

Factors That Affect Siding Cost

Several variables beyond material choice influence your final siding replacement bill.

Home size and stories. A larger home requires more material, and multi-story homes require scaffolding or lifts that add $500 to $2,000 to the project cost. Second and third story work is slower due to setup time and safety considerations, increasing labor costs by 20 to 30 percent compared to single-story work.

Architectural complexity. Homes with many corners, dormers, bump-outs, gables, and trim details require significantly more cutting, fitting, and trim work. A simple ranch-style home with four flat walls costs less per square foot than a Victorian with intricate trim, multiple gable ends, and curved surfaces.

Existing siding removal. If the old siding contains asbestos (common in homes built before 1980), removal requires certified abatement contractors and adds $2,000 to $8,000+ to the project. Even standard removal adds $0.50 to $1.50 per square foot for tear-off, cleanup, and disposal.

Underlying damage. Once old siding is removed, rot, water damage, or insect damage to the sheathing or framing may be discovered. Repairing structural damage adds $500 to $5,000+ depending on the extent. Replacing deteriorated housewrap and installing proper moisture barriers adds $0.50 to $1.00 per square foot but is essential for the new siding to perform correctly.

Insulation upgrades. Adding rigid foam insulation board (like Thermax or ZIP-R) beneath new siding dramatically improves energy efficiency and can qualify for tax credits. Insulation board adds $1 to $3 per square foot but often pays for itself in reduced heating and cooling costs within five to seven years. This is a perfect complement to attic and wall insulation upgrades.

Signs You Need New Siding

Siding deterioration often starts small but escalates quickly once moisture penetrates the wall cavity.

Warping, buckling, or loose panels. Siding that no longer lies flat against the wall is failing. Warped vinyl indicates heat damage or improper installation. Buckling wood siding suggests moisture absorption. Loose panels allow wind-driven rain behind the siding.

Rot or soft spots. Press the end of a screwdriver into suspicious areas. If the material is soft, crumbly, or shows signs of fungal growth, the siding has failed and moisture has likely reached the wall cavity behind it. Rot spreads rapidly once established.

Rising energy bills. If your heating and cooling costs have gradually increased despite maintaining your HVAC system, deteriorated siding may be allowing air infiltration and heat transfer through compromised wall cavities.

Paint peeling or bubbling. If exterior paint peels within two to three years of application, moisture is likely migrating through the siding from inside the wall cavity. Repainting will not fix this problem; the underlying moisture source must be addressed, often by replacing the siding and improving moisture management.

Visible mold or mildew. While surface mold can be cleaned, persistent or recurring mold growth indicates moisture problems behind the siding. This is especially concerning on north-facing walls that receive less sun and stay damp longer.

Red Flags When Hiring Siding Contractors

Siding is a significant investment that should be done correctly the first time. Watch for these warning signs.

No manufacturer certification. James Hardie and LP SmartSide both have preferred installer programs. Certified installers have completed manufacturer training and can offer enhanced warranties. Non-certified installers may void the manufacturer's warranty through improper installation techniques.

Skipping housewrap or flashing. Proper moisture management behind siding is critical. Any contractor who plans to install new siding directly over existing sheathing without installing or verifying housewrap and proper window and door flashing is setting you up for moisture problems and potential structural damage.

No detailed written estimate. A professional siding estimate should itemize removal, preparation, materials (with specific product names and colors), labor, trim work, cleanup, and warranty terms. Vague estimates like "siding job, $8,000" do not provide enough detail to compare quotes or hold the contractor accountable.

Pressure to decide immediately. Reputable siding contractors provide written estimates and give you time to compare options. High-pressure tactics, "today only" pricing, and refusal to provide references are signs of an unreliable contractor.

Money-Saving Tips

Smart strategies to reduce siding costs without compromising quality or longevity.

Get multiple quotes. Siding estimates vary dramatically between contractors. Get at least three detailed quotes and compare the scope of work, materials specified, and warranty coverage. The cheapest quote is not always the best value if it uses thinner materials or skips critical preparation steps.

Time your project strategically. Fall and winter are typically slower seasons for siding contractors in most regions. Scheduling during these off-peak periods can save 10 to 20 percent on labor as contractors compete for fewer available jobs.

Consider mixing materials. Using premium fiber cement on the front facade and more affordable vinyl on the sides and rear saves significantly while maintaining curb appeal. This approach is common and provides the visual impact of premium materials where it matters most.

Bundle with other exterior work. If you also need new gutters, windows, or roofing, bundling these projects with siding saves money on scaffolding, permits, and contractor mobilization. Many exterior contractors offer package discounts for multi-system projects.

Check for manufacturer rebates. James Hardie and LP SmartSide periodically offer rebate programs for homeowners. Check manufacturer websites for current promotions before finalizing your material choice.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does siding last? Vinyl siding lasts 20 to 40 years, fiber cement lasts 40 to 50+ years, engineered wood lasts 30 to 50 years, and natural wood lasts 20 to 40 years with regular maintenance. These lifespans assume proper installation and reasonable maintenance.

Can new siding go over old siding? In some cases, yes. Vinyl siding can be installed over existing vinyl or wood siding if the underlying structure is sound. However, this approach prevents inspection of the sheathing for damage, can create moisture trapping issues, and adds thickness that may require extending window and door trim. Complete removal and fresh installation is generally the better approach.

Does new siding increase home value? Yes. Siding replacement consistently ranks among the top home improvement projects for return on investment, typically recouping 60 to 80 percent of the project cost at resale. Fiber cement siding offers the highest ROI among siding materials. Beyond resale value, new siding improves energy efficiency, reduces maintenance costs, and dramatically enhances curb appeal.

Do I need a permit for siding replacement? Requirements vary by jurisdiction. Many cities and counties require permits for full siding replacement, especially if the project involves structural repairs, insulation changes, or modifications to the building envelope. Partial repairs (replacing a few damaged panels) typically do not require permits. Check with your local building department before starting the project.
