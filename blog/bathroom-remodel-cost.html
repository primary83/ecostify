Bathroom Remodel Cost 2026: Budget to Luxury

Updated February 2026 · 12 min read

A bathroom remodel is one of the smartest investments you can make in your home. Whether you are updating a cramped half bath, refreshing a dated hall bathroom, or building the master bath of your dreams, knowing what things actually cost in 2026 will help you set a realistic budget, avoid surprise invoices, and get the best value for every dollar you spend. This guide covers every cost variable from tile and toilets to labor rates and permit fees so you can plan your project with confidence.

Quick Cost Summary

Bathroom remodel costs vary widely depending on the size of the room, the scope of work, and your material choices. Here is a snapshot of what homeowners are paying nationally in 2026.

Half-Bath Refresh (paint, fixtures, vanity) $3,500 — $7,000
Full Bathroom — Cosmetic Update $6,000 — $12,000
Full Bathroom — Mid-Range Remodel $12,000 — $22,000
Master Bath — Mid-Range Remodel $18,000 — $30,000
Master Bath — Luxury Renovation $35,000 — $75,000+

The national average for a standard full-bathroom remodel in 2026 sits around $15,000 to $18,000. Most homeowners spend between $10,000 and $25,000 once all materials, labor, and incidentals are factored in. Your final number will depend heavily on whether you keep the existing layout or move plumbing, which is the single biggest cost driver in any bathroom project.

Half Bath vs. Full Bath vs. Master Bath

The type of bathroom you are remodeling determines the baseline complexity and cost of the project. Understanding the differences helps you set the right expectations before you start calling contractors.

A half bath (also called a powder room) contains only a toilet and a sink. Because there is no shower or tub, plumbing work is minimal and the square footage is small, typically 15 to 25 square feet. A half-bath refresh with a new vanity, faucet, toilet, paint, mirror, and lighting runs $3,500 to $7,000. If you add new flooring and tile wainscoting, expect $5,000 to $9,000.

A full bathroom includes a toilet, sink, and either a shower, a bathtub, or a tub-shower combo. These rooms average 35 to 50 square feet. Full-bath remodels involve more plumbing, more tile, and more labor, which pushes costs into the $6,000 to $22,000 range depending on scope. Swapping out a tub-shower combo for a modern walk-in shower is one of the most popular upgrades in this category.

A master bathroom is typically the largest bathroom in the home, often 60 to 120 square feet or more. Master baths frequently include double vanities, a separate walk-in shower and freestanding tub, and premium finishes. The extra square footage, dual plumbing connections, and higher-end materials mean master bath renovations range from $18,000 for a mid-range refresh to $75,000 or more for a luxury gut renovation with heated floors and frameless glass.

Cost Breakdown by Component

Knowing where the money goes in a bathroom remodel helps you decide where to splurge, where to save, and where to expect the unexpected. Here is a component-by-component breakdown of typical costs for a standard full-bathroom remodel.

Tile and Shower Walls

Ceramic tile (floor and walls) $2 — $8 / sq ft
Porcelain tile $3 — $12 / sq ft
Natural stone (marble, slate, travertine) $10 — $30 / sq ft
Large-format porcelain panels $15 — $40 / sq ft
Tile installation labor $6 — $15 / sq ft

Tile is typically the second largest material cost after the vanity or shower system. For a standard shower surround of roughly 70 to 90 square feet of wall tile, homeowners spend $1,200 to $3,500 on materials alone. Subway tile remains the most cost-effective option for a clean, classic look. Large-format porcelain panels are trending in 2026 for their seamless appearance and reduced grout maintenance, but they cost significantly more and require experienced installers. For a deeper breakdown of all flooring options, see our flooring installation cost guide.

Vanity and Countertop

Stock vanity (single sink, 24–36 in.) $200 — $800
Semi-custom vanity (single or double sink) $800 — $2,500
Custom vanity with stone top $2,500 — $6,000+
Double vanity (60–72 in.) $1,200 — $4,500
Vanity installation labor $200 — $500

A stock vanity with an integrated top from a home improvement store is the fastest and most affordable option for budget remodels. If you want a custom look without the custom price, a floating vanity with a quartz top from a semi-custom manufacturer hits a great balance between style and value. For master baths, a double vanity is practically a requirement for resale value in most markets.

Toilet

Basic two-piece toilet $150 — $350
One-piece comfort-height toilet $300 — $700
Wall-hung toilet $500 — $1,500
Smart toilet (bidet seat, heated, self-cleaning) $1,500 — $5,000
Toilet installation labor $150 — $350

For most remodels, a quality one-piece comfort-height toilet in the $350 to $500 range offers the best value. Brands like TOTO, Kohler, and American Standard dominate this segment. Wall-hung toilets look sleek and make floor cleaning easier, but they require an in-wall carrier frame that adds $300 to $600 in additional installation costs. Smart toilets with integrated bidet seats are growing in popularity in 2026, especially in master bath renovations.

Shower and Tub

Acrylic tub-shower combo (prefab) $400 — $1,200
Tiled walk-in shower (custom) $3,500 — $9,000
Frameless glass shower enclosure $1,200 — $3,500
Freestanding soaking tub $800 — $4,000
Shower valve and trim kit $200 — $800
Shower/tub installation labor $1,000 — $3,500

The shower or tub is the centerpiece of any bathroom remodel and often the biggest single line item. A basic tub-to-shower conversion using a prefabricated acrylic base and tile walls typically costs $4,000 to $8,000 all-in. A fully custom tiled walk-in shower with a linear drain, bench seat, niche, and frameless glass door runs $7,000 to $14,000 for a mid-range finish. Luxury walk-in showers with body sprays, rain heads, and natural stone can exceed $20,000.

Plumbing

Fixture hookup (no relocation) $500 — $1,500
Relocating drain or supply lines $1,500 — $5,000
New shower valve rough-in $400 — $900
Moving a toilet location $800 — $3,000

Plumbing is where bathroom remodels get expensive fast. If you keep fixtures in their existing locations, plumbing costs stay relatively low at $500 to $1,500 total. The moment you move a toilet, shift a shower drain, or add a second sink, costs escalate quickly because the contractor needs to open up floors and walls to reroute drain lines. On a concrete slab foundation, moving plumbing is even more expensive because it requires cutting and patching the slab.

Electrical

GFCI outlet installation $150 — $300 each
Vanity light fixture installation $150 — $400
Exhaust fan (with vent to exterior) $200 — $500
Recessed lighting (per light) $150 — $300
Heated floor thermostat and wiring $300 — $700

Electrical upgrades are often required in older bathrooms that lack proper GFCI protection, adequate ventilation, or sufficient lighting circuits. A modern bathroom should have at least one dedicated 20-amp circuit, GFCI-protected outlets near the vanity, and a properly vented exhaust fan. If your home was built before the 1990s, expect to budget $800 to $2,000 for electrical updates to bring everything up to current code. Check your local requirements, as most municipalities require permits for any new electrical work. Flooring

Vinyl sheet or LVP (waterproof) $3 — $7 / sq ft installed
Ceramic tile $6 — $14 / sq ft installed
Porcelain tile $8 — $18 / sq ft installed
Heated floor mat system $8 — $15 / sq ft (materials)

Bathroom floors must be waterproof or at least highly water-resistant. Porcelain tile is the gold standard for bathroom flooring because it absorbs almost no moisture and lasts decades. Luxury vinyl plank is an increasingly popular alternative that is warmer underfoot, easier to install, and significantly cheaper. For a 40-square-foot bathroom, flooring materials and installation typically run $400 to $900 with vinyl or $600 to $1,400 with porcelain tile. Adding electric radiant heated floor mats underneath tile costs an extra $400 to $800 for a standard bathroom and is one of the most popular upgrades in 2026 master bath renovations.

Labor Costs for Bathroom Remodels

Labor accounts for 40 to 60 percent of a typical bathroom remodel budget. In 2026, skilled trades remain in high demand, and labor rates reflect that. Here is what you can expect to pay for the key trades involved in a bathroom project.

General contractor (project management) 15 — 25% of total project cost
Plumber $80 — $150 / hour
Electrician $75 — $130 / hour
Tile installer $6 — $15 / sq ft
Carpenter / finish work $50 — $100 / hour
Painter $35 — $70 / hour

Labor rates vary significantly by region. Major metro areas like New York, San Francisco, and Boston run 30 to 50 percent higher than the national average, while smaller cities and rural areas are often 10 to 20 percent below average. Getting at least three detailed quotes from licensed contractors is essential for understanding what labor costs look like in your specific market. Budget vs. Mid-Range vs. Luxury: What You Get

Budget Remodel ($3,500 to $10,000)

A budget bathroom remodel focuses on cosmetic improvements without moving any plumbing. Typical projects include repainting walls and ceiling, replacing the vanity with a stock unit, swapping out the toilet, installing a new mirror and light fixture, adding a new faucet and showerhead, and replacing the flooring with vinyl or budget ceramic tile. You keep the existing tub or shower in place and refresh its appearance with reglazing ($300 to $600) or a prefab acrylic surround ($500 to $1,200). A budget remodel can dramatically improve the look and feel of an outdated bathroom without breaking the bank.

Mid-Range Remodel ($10,000 to $25,000)

A mid-range remodel involves replacing most or all fixtures and finishes while keeping the same general layout. This tier typically includes a new tiled shower or tub surround, semi-custom vanity with a stone or quartz top, comfort-height toilet, porcelain tile flooring, updated lighting with recessed cans and a modern vanity light, new exhaust fan, GFCI outlets, and fresh paint. You may also upgrade to a frameless glass shower door and add features like a tile niche or heated flooring. Mid-range remodels deliver the best balance of value and visual impact for most homeowners.

Luxury Remodel ($25,000 to $75,000+)

Luxury master bath renovations pull out all the stops. These projects often involve expanding the bathroom footprint, moving plumbing for an optimized layout, and specifying premium materials throughout. Common features include oversized walk-in showers with multiple showerheads and body sprays, freestanding soaking tubs, custom double vanities with natural stone countertops, heated porcelain or natural stone flooring, smart toilets, custom cabinetry, designer tile or stone accent walls, and high-end lighting with dimmers. Luxury renovations also frequently add linen closets, dressing areas, or water closets (enclosed toilet rooms) within the master bath.

Walk-In Shower Cost Deep Dive

Converting a bathtub to a walk-in shower is the single most popular bathroom renovation project in 2026. Homeowners are drawn to walk-in showers for their accessibility, ease of cleaning, modern appearance, and the perception of a larger, more open bathroom. Here is what the conversion costs by approach.

Prefab acrylic shower pan + tile walls $4,000 — $7,000
Fully custom tile shower (mortar bed or foam pan) $7,000 — $14,000
Curbless (zero-threshold) walk-in shower $9,000 — $18,000
Frameless glass enclosure (add-on) $1,200 — $3,500

A curbless walk-in shower is the premium option that provides a spa-like feel and full ADA accessibility. However, curbless showers require careful waterproofing and precise floor slope engineering, which is why they cost significantly more than a standard curbed shower. The floor of the entire bathroom typically needs to be rebuilt to accommodate the drainage slope, especially if you want a linear drain along one wall. If your budget is limited, a standard curbed walk-in shower with a frameless glass panel delivers 90 percent of the visual impact at 50 to 60 percent of the cost.

DIY vs. Hiring a Professional

Some bathroom tasks are well-suited for handy homeowners, while others require licensed professionals for both safety and code compliance. Here is how to decide what to tackle yourself and what to leave to the pros.

Good DIY projects: painting, installing a new mirror, swapping out a toilet (if you are comfortable with plumbing connections), replacing faucets and showerheads, installing vanity light fixtures (if wiring exists), removing old flooring and tile for demolition savings, and installing vinyl plank flooring.

Hire a pro for: any work involving moving or adding plumbing supply or drain lines, electrical panel work or adding new circuits, tile installation on shower walls and floors (waterproofing is critical), shower pan construction and waterproofing membrane installation, structural changes like moving walls or enlarging doorways, and any work that requires a building permit.

A DIY-friendly bathroom refresh that combines your own labor for demolition, painting, and simple fixture swaps with professional help for plumbing and tile can save 20 to 35 percent compared to hiring a general contractor for the entire project. However, poorly executed DIY waterproofing in a shower is one of the most expensive mistakes a homeowner can make. Water leaks behind shower walls cause mold, rot, and structural damage that can cost $5,000 to $15,000 to repair. When in doubt, pay a professional tile installer to handle the shower.

Permits and Inspections

Most municipalities require permits for bathroom remodels that involve plumbing changes, new electrical circuits, or structural modifications. A standard bathroom remodel permit costs $100 to $500 in most areas, though complex projects in some cities can require permits costing $1,000 or more. Inspections are typically required at the rough-in stage (before walls are closed up) and again at final completion.

Skipping permits might seem like a way to save money and time, but it carries serious risks. Unpermitted work can void your homeowner's insurance coverage if water damage occurs, create legal complications when you sell your home, result in fines if discovered during a future inspection, and force you to tear out finished work so an inspector can verify the rough plumbing and electrical. Always ask your contractor whether permits are required for your specific scope of work, and be wary of any contractor who suggests skipping them.

Bathroom Remodel Timeline

Understanding the timeline helps you plan around the disruption of losing a bathroom. Here is what to expect for each project scope.

Half-bath refresh (cosmetic only) 3 — 5 days
Full bath — cosmetic update 1 — 2 weeks
Full bath — mid-range remodel 3 — 5 weeks
Master bath — mid-range remodel 4 — 7 weeks
Master bath — luxury renovation 8 — 14 weeks

These timelines assume materials are on hand and permits are approved before work begins. Custom vanities have lead times of 4 to 8 weeks. Specialty tile or stone may need 2 to 6 weeks for delivery. Frameless glass shower enclosures are measured after tile is complete and take 2 to 3 weeks to fabricate. Build these lead times into your planning so your contractor is not sitting idle waiting for materials. If you are remodeling your only bathroom, discuss a phased approach with your contractor so the toilet and a temporary shower remain functional throughout most of the project.

Return on Investment

Bathroom remodels consistently rank among the top home improvement projects for ROI. A mid-range bathroom remodel recoups approximately 60 to 70 percent of its cost at resale, while a budget-friendly update focused on cosmetic improvements can return 70 to 80 percent. Luxury renovations tend to return a lower percentage (40 to 55 percent) because high-end finishes are subjective and buyers may not value your specific material choices.

The highest-ROI bathroom improvements include replacing an old vanity with a modern unit, adding a walk-in shower, updating dated tile, and improving lighting. The key is proportionality. A bathroom remodel should cost no more than 5 to 10 percent of your home's total value to maximize return. Over-improving a bathroom relative to your home's price point and neighborhood is one of the most common ways homeowners lose money on renovations. If your home is valued at $350,000, spending $75,000 on a master bath is unlikely to produce a positive return.

Money-Saving Tips

  • Keep the existing layout — Avoiding plumbing relocation is the single biggest way to cut costs. Moving a toilet or shower drain can add $2,000 to $5,000 to your budget.
  • Choose porcelain tile that looks like natural stone — Modern porcelain tiles convincingly mimic marble, travertine, and slate at a fraction of the price and with zero maintenance.
  • Buy a stock vanity and upgrade the top — Pair a $300 stock vanity with a $400 quartz top for a semi-custom look at half the price of a true semi-custom unit.
  • Do your own demolition — Ripping out old tile, vanities, and flooring yourself saves $500 to $1,500 in labor. Just be careful around plumbing and electrical.
  • Time your project for the off-season — Contractors are typically less busy from November through February. You may get better pricing and faster scheduling during these months.
  • Refinish instead of replacing the tub — Professional bathtub reglazing costs $300 to $600 and makes an old porcelain tub look brand new for another 10 to 15 years.
  • Shop clearance and overstock tile — Tile stores and big-box retailers frequently clearance discontinued tile patterns at 40 to 60 percent off. If you can find enough square footage for your project, the savings are substantial.
  • Skip the heated floors in small bathrooms — A quality bath mat provides the same comfort for a fraction of the $800 to $1,500 radiant floor installation cost in a small space.

Red Flags When Hiring a Bathroom Contractor

Hiring the wrong contractor is the fastest way to turn a bathroom remodel into a nightmare. Watch out for these warning signs during the bidding and hiring process.

  • Demands more than 30 percent upfront — Reputable contractors typically ask for a 10 to 15 percent deposit, with progress payments tied to milestones. A contractor who wants 50 percent upfront before buying any materials is a major red flag.
  • No written contract or vague scope of work — Every detail should be spelled out in writing: materials, brands, quantities, labor costs, payment schedule, start date, projected completion date, and warranty terms.
  • Cannot provide proof of license and insurance — A licensed contractor carries general liability insurance and workers' compensation. Ask for certificates and verify them with the insurance carrier.
  • Suggests skipping permits — Any contractor who tells you permits are unnecessary for plumbing or electrical work is either uninformed or cutting corners. Either way, walk away.
  • No references or online reviews — A reputable bathroom contractor should have a portfolio of completed projects and reviews on Google, Yelp, or Angi. No track record means you are taking a gamble.
  • Quote is significantly lower than all other bids — If one bid comes in 30 to 40 percent below the others, it almost always means cheaper materials, unlicensed subcontractors, or change orders that will inflate the final price.
  • Pressure to sign immediately — Legitimate contractors give you time to review the contract, check references, and compare bids. High-pressure sales tactics are a hallmark of less reputable operators.
